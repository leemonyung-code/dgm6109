Visualization: Study Time vs. Delayed Recall

Research Question & Hypothesis

Question: How does the amount of time spent studying (in minutes) affect the Delayed Recall Score (measured 24 hours later)? Does this relationship vary depending on the study method used?

Hypothesis: As study time increases, the delayed recall score will generally increase. I also hypothesize that "Spaced Repetition" and "Mnemonic Imagery" will show higher efficiency (better scores for the same time) compared to "Massed Practice".

Data Overview

The data presented here was collected over a period of 4 weeks (October 1st to October 28th, 2025). Each data point represents a daily study session.

  • Collection Frequency: Once per day.
  • Key Variables:
    • Study Time: Duration of the session in minutes (X-axis).
    • Delayed Recall Score: A test score out of 15 points, taken 24 hours after studying (Y-axis).
    • Method: The technique used (Spaced Repetition, Massed Practice, Elaborative Encoding, Mnemonic Imagery), represented by color.
  • Completeness: The dataset includes 28 consecutive days of records, satisfying the requirement for 4+ weeks of data.

Conclusion

Based on the visualization, there appears to be a general positive correlation between study time and delayed recall score, supporting the initial hypothesis. However, the method of study plays a significant role. "Massed Practice" data points (orange) tend to cluster lower on the Y-axis despite varying study times, suggesting it is less effective. Conversely, "Mnemonic Imagery" (red) and "Elaborative Encoding" (green) show consistently higher recall scores. This suggests that while time is a factor, the quality or technique of study is perhaps a stronger predictor of retention than time alone.
