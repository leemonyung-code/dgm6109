Visualization: Study Time vs. Delayed Recall

Hypothesis & Question

Question: How does different study method and the duration of a study time affect my performance to recall vocabs 24 hours later?

Hypothesis: I hypothesize that there will be a positive correlation between study time and delayed recall score. However, I also predict that active study methods (like Mnemonic Imagery) will get higher retention rates than passive methods (like Massed Practice), regardless of the time spent.

Data Overview

The data presented here was collected over a period of 10 weeks (October 1st to December 10th, 2025). Each data point represents a daily study session.

  • Collection Frequency: Once per day.
  • Key Variables:
    • Study Time: Duration of the session in minutes (X-axis).
    • Delayed Recall Score: A test score out of 15 points, taken 24 hours after studying (Y-axis).
    • Method: The technique used, represented by color (4 categories).
    • Self-Rated Confidence: A score (7-9.0) representing how confident I felt, represented by circle size.
  • Completeness: The dataset includes consistent daily records until the end of the term, satisfying the requirement for long-term data collection.

Conclusion

Based on the visualization of the full term's data, my hypothesis is strongly supported. "Mnemonic Imagery" (Yellow) consistently yields the highest Recall Scores (clustering at the top-left and top-center), indicating high efficiency even with moderate study time. Conversely, "Massed Practice" (Blue) points cluster at the bottom, showing low retention rates (often below 60%) regardless of how long I studied. This suggests that the method of study is a more significant predictor of performance than the raw duration (Study Time).
